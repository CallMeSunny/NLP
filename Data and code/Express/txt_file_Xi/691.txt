    The Australian trade minister Steven Ciobo is due to meet UK ministers in London next week to kick the process off. The summit concluded with a 30 minute bilateral meeting between Mrs May and President Xi who told her he is "entirely open to a bilateral trading relationship" with the UK. Mrs May agreed to visit the country again next year and President Xi said that the meeting had "provided solid groundwork" for a future relationship.   PAMrs May held a meeting with Chinese President Xi Jinping at the State GuesthouseHe also made it clear he understood Mrs May's delay over agreeing an £18 billion Hinkley Point nuclear power station project where China is the main investor. He assured her that the Chinese Government will be "patient" while she makes her decision.Earlier, at her press conference in Hangzhou, Mrs May said: "What I found pleasing and very useful in the discussions I had, particularly in bilaterals with a number of world leaders, is the willingness to talk to us about opening up trade arrangements between the United Kingdom and a number of other countries."    We were both very clear that we will be working together to ensure that we can maintain and build on our relationshipTheresa MayShe also dismissed concerns about Japan pulling its investment from Britain including the Nissan car factory in the North East of England.She said: "We were both very clear that we will be working together to ensure that we can maintain and build on our relationship."And if you think about it, the single biggest vote of confidence in the UK in terms of inward investment, since the referendum, was from a Japanese company, from Softbank and its 24 billion (pound) proposal to buy ARM."   REUTERSMrs May praised the willingness of world leaders to talk about potential trade dealsMr Turnbull said on Monday he wants to negotiate a "very strong" free trade agreement with Britain after it leaves the European Union. Britain's June decision to leave the 28-country EU sent financial markets into shock in anticipation of a recession as Britain enters a years-long process of tearing itself away from its biggest trading partner and forging a new global economic role.   GETTYMalcolm Turnbull reiterated his commitment to a quick "strong" trade deal with BritainThe Australian Prime Minister told reporters that May told the Australians she "remains very grateful for the assistance we are providing, both legislative and in terms of other resources". "And of course, from our point of view, getting in to deal with the British early and making sure we can negotiate a very strong, very open free trade agreement once they are actually out of the European Union."
